PROJECT SUMMARY

We decided to address the topic of climate change by setting our scope to sustainability in the context of clothing consumption amongst college students. Focusing on how the process creates the product, my team and I conducted extensive research and analysis to produce a mobile application prototype that allows users to swap, sell, and buy second-hand items with people in their area and beyond.

USER RESEARCH

After our initial stakeholder conversations and secondary research, I collaborated with a team member to write our stakeholder interview questions. Our team gathered interview data for 10 people and online field observation data for 8 people. We also collected in-person field observation data at a local mall and a campus clothing swap event.

Key insight: There are social conventions about fashion that compel people, particularly women, to continually be wearing items that are new and trendy. Social media and influencer culture play a significant role in promoting consumerism and over-consumption, especially to younger generations who are highly active on social media.

ANALYSIS

We came together to discuss the dissect the data by first sharing what we learned during our research. Getting an idea of the types of people our stakeholders were, I came up with 6 personas. My team members also constructed the "Day in the Life" and "Identity" design models to contextualize stakeholder behaviors. Then we came together to categorize all of our interview and field observation data by topics within affinity diagrams. This analysis helped to set the scene for defining problems we were looking to solve.

PROBLEM

We received a spectrum of answers but overall, our stakeholders had a general sense of the issues with fast fashion. However, accessibility constraints outweighed how peoples' knowledge affected their actions. More than sustainability, stakeholders prioritized price, convenience, and style. Moreover, most companies only offered fast fashion choices and others are suspect to greenwashing.

Design challenge: How might we integrate sustainability into routine shopping practices of stakeholders?

SOLUTION

To brainstorm design solutions, our team utilized the crazy eights design sprint method. We storyboarded use-cases of our top 4 chosen solutions. We ended up choosing an idea that I came up with- a clothing swap app. My solution was based on addressing the culture around clothing consumption; I believe consumerism and over-consumption are linked to cultural trends and if we can shift the culture, we can solve the problem. I tied this to a user interface I had seen trending amongst our demographic, Tinder, redirecting the excitement and interest of swiping to clothes.

PROTOTYPING AND TESTING

We drew wireframes and converged upon a lo-fi prototype, on which we conducted concept testing. We then created a mid-fi prototype and conducted usability testing. We iterated our mid-fi prototype to conduct validation testing. From this, we produced our hi-fi prototype. In total, we created 4 design iterations and completed 3 rounds of user testing with 8 stakeholders.

NAVIGATION

After onboarding, the user was originally brought to their profile page. However, users had a mental model of the profile tab being the rightmost on a navbar. To follow these conventions, as well as highlight the key feature of our app, we moved the swap tab to the leftmost spot, making it the landing page.

SWAP VERSUS SHOP

Although we introduced swapping clothes as our key feature, we still wanted users to have to option to buy and sell clothes. Yet through our usability testing, we found users to confused with these distinctions. Therefore, we clarified through the use of tags. When listing an item, users are prompted to enter whether they want to swap their item, sell their item, or do both. As such, these tags will appear on images of items searched for in the shop section as well as the user's profile. When conversing with another user about obtaining an item, instead of multiple item images signifying a swap, the tags will appear under the username.
